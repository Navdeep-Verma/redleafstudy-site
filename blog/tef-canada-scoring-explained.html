TEF Canada Scoring Explained

Updated July 2026 · 5 min read

TEF Canada's scoring system trips people up for a simple reason: unlike IELTS's clean 0-9 band scale, TEF Canada scores each of its four skills on its own separate numeric scale, with no combined total at all. Understanding how those numbers become the NCLC level IRCC actually uses is the key to knowing what you're aiming for.

Quick answer: Reading, listening, writing, and speaking are each scored independently, then converted to NCLC (the French-language equivalent of CLB). NCLC 7 in all four skills is the most common target for Express Entry's Federal Skilled Worker category and the French-language category.

Why there's no single "TEF score"

Each of the four skills — reading, listening, writing, and speaking — is assessed and scored on its own scale, and IRCC converts each one to an NCLC level independently rather than averaging them together. That means your weakest skill effectively sets your usable level for immigration purposes, even if your other three skills are much stronger. If you're aiming for NCLC 7 across the board, you genuinely need to track four separate targets, not one overall number.

Important: TEF Canada's exact numeric conversion tables have changed over time, and different sources online currently show different specific number ranges for the same NCLC levels — some of this reflects an older scoring scale versus a newer one introduced more recently. Rather than risk repeating an outdated or mismatched figure here, use IRCC's official, current TEF Canada conversion table directly at canada.ca when checking your own results, and double-check which score column (the current scale versus any legacy equivalency column) your official results actually report.

What NCLC actually measures

NCLC (Niveaux de compétence linguistique canadiens) is the French-language counterpart to the English CLB scale, running from roughly 1 (very basic) to the highest levels representing near-native proficiency. Canadian immigration programs set minimum NCLC requirements the same way they set CLB requirements for English tests — often per skill, not as one blended average.

Why French matters even outside Quebec

A strong TEF Canada result doesn't just satisfy French-specific programs — it can meaningfully increase your Express Entry Comprehensive Ranking System score even if your primary application isn't Quebec-focused, and it opens eligibility for French-language category draws, which have at times invited candidates at CRS scores well below general draw cut-offs.

How to prepare, given the four-skills structure

  • Diagnose your weakest skill first. Since your lowest skill caps your usable NCLC level, an hour spent improving your weakest area is worth more than an hour polishing a skill that's already comfortably above your target.
  • Practice under real test conditions for listening specifically. TEF Canada's listening section typically plays each recording only once, with no option to replay — this format detail alone is worth practicing deliberately, since it's different from casual listening practice.
  • Track four separate numbers, not one. Keep a simple record of your practice-test performance in each of the four skills separately, rather than one combined "how am I doing" impression.
