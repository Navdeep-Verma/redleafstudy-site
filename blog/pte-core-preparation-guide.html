PTE Core Preparation Guide

Updated July 2026 · 6 min read

PTE Core is the newest of the major English tests accepted for Canadian immigration, and its format works differently from IELTS or CELPIP in one important way: the tasks are integrated, not separated into four clean boxes. A single question can test more than one skill at once, which surprises test-takers who prepare each skill in isolation.

Quick answer: 3 sections (Speaking & Writing, Reading, Listening), about 2 hours total, roughly 19 question types, scored 10-90 overall and per skill, fully AI-scored with human evaluation, results valid 2 years for immigration purposes.

Why "integrated" tasks change how you should prepare

Traditional test prep often treats reading, writing, listening, and speaking as four separate subjects to study one at a time. PTE Core doesn't test that way — a reading passage might feed into a speaking response, and a listening clip might require a written summary. Studying skills in isolation only gets you partway there; you also need practice moving between skills within a single task, under time pressure.

The three sections

  • Speaking & Writing: includes tasks like Read Aloud, Repeat Sentence, Describe Image, Summarize Written Text, and Write Essay — plus newer task types such as Summarize Group Discussion and Respond to a Situation.
  • Reading: multiple choice, reading fill-in-the-blanks, and reorder paragraphs — a task type fairly unique to PTE, where you arrange scrambled sentences into a logical order.
  • Listening: summarize spoken text, fill-in-the-blanks while listening, highlighting the correct summary, and similar comprehension tasks.

How scoring actually works

Your result is a single overall score from 10 to 90, broken down into four skill scores on the same scale. Scoring is automated, checking things like content accuracy, grammar, vocabulary, spelling, and — for spoken tasks — pronunciation and fluency, with human evaluation involved as well rather than AI scoring completely alone. One detail worth knowing: PTE Core is not adaptive. Every test-taker sees a similarly structured test rather than one that changes difficulty based on your answers.

Preparation strategies that actually help

  • Learn all 19 question types individually first. Generic English-practice materials won't prepare you for PTE Core's specific task formats — timing and response rules differ from what you'd expect from general study content.
  • Practice with a strict timer. Both the overall section and many individual questions have their own time limits, and the exam won't wait for you to gather your thoughts.
  • Prioritize high-value task types. Tasks like Write from Dictation and Fill in the Blanks tend to have an outsized effect on your score relative to how quickly they can be mastered with practice.
  • Practice speaking into a microphone, not just to yourself. The recorded, no-examiner format rewards a specific kind of clear, steady delivery that's worth rehearsing in the actual format you'll be tested in.

Is PTE Core right for you?

If you want a modern, fully computer-and-AI-scored test with a wide network of test centres and no live speaking interview, PTE Core is a strong option — especially if you're comfortable with integrated, fast-paced tasks rather than cleanly separated sections.
