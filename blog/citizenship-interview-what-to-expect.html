Canadian Citizenship Interview: What to Expect

Updated July 2026 · 4 min read

Not every applicant is called for an interview — it typically comes up if there are questions about your application, or if you didn't pass the written knowledge test after two attempts. Here's what the process generally looks like.

Quick answer: a citizenship interview or hearing is a conversation with a citizenship official, typically 30–90 minutes, that can happen by video call or in person. It's meant to verify your eligibility, not to trip you up.

Who conducts it

Depending on your file, this may be a citizenship officer or a citizenship judge. Either way, their role is to verify the information in your application and, where relevant, assess your knowledge of Canada directly through conversation rather than a written test.

What it's actually like

Despite the formal-sounding name, applicants who've been through it commonly describe it as conversational rather than like a formal exam. You may be asked about your physical presence in Canada, your application details, and general knowledge questions drawn from the same official study guide the written test uses.

How to prepare

  • Review your own application before the interview — know your dates of entry, exit, and residence history well, since these are commonly discussed.
  • Go over the same core study material as the written test: rights and responsibilities, government structure, history, and symbols.
  • Practice explaining concepts in your own words rather than just recognizing the right multiple-choice answer — interviews reward being able to talk through an idea, not just recall it.
  • If your English or French is a factor, practicing conversationally (not just reading) can help you feel more at ease.

What happens afterward

You'll typically be notified of the outcome by letter. If the interview goes well, you'll move forward toward the citizenship ceremony. If there are still concerns about your eligibility, your application could be refused at this stage, at which point reapplying is generally the next step if you want to continue pursuing citizenship.

Build real understanding, not just memorized answers

Detailed chapter notes explain the "why" behind each fact — which helps far more in an interview setting than flashcards alone.
